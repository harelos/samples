Your dog cannot tell you what is wrong. So the signals everyone misses matter more.

The slow dips owners write off as ‘just getting older’ are often the exact ones that would answer to something simple, if anyone were reading them.

The hardest part of loving a dog is that it cannot tell you. It cannot say the joint aches on the stairs now, or that the itching keeps it up, or that its stomach has been off for a week. It just keeps wagging, keeps showing up, keeps trying to be the dog you love, and quietly absorbs whatever is wrong.

So the signals get quiet and easy to miss. A little slower to the door. Scratching that became background noise months ago. A coat that lost some shine. Sleeping a bit more. And because each one is small, and because dogs are stoic in a way that breaks your heart if you think about it too long, we file them all under the same heading: just getting older.

Sometimes that is true. But sit with a good vet and you learn how often it is not. How often the slowing and the itching and the gut trouble are not the fixed price of age but the visible tip of something specific. Something with a name. Something that, caught early, actually responds.

Dogs do not get to complain their way to a diagnosis. Which means the small stuff we ignore is often the only warning we are ever going to get.

Here is where the pet aisle quietly fails the people trying hardest. Walk it and you find the kitchen-sink chew: thirty ingredients on the label, a picture of a happy dog, and a dusting of each active so light it functions as decoration. It is built to look like it does everything, which is a very effective way to do almost nothing.

Because a dog that is stiff on the stairs and a dog that cannot stop scratching do not have the same problem, and no single everything-chew serves both honestly. The interesting question is the one the shelf refuses to ask: what if you matched the actual signal your dog is showing to a formula built, and dosed, for that one thing?

What that looks like in practice, and the difference between a real dose and a label full of hopeful sprinkles, is where reading your dog finally turns into helping it.
