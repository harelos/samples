Two thirds of Alzheimer’s patients are women. Almost none of the research budget knows it.

A mother and son looked at where brain-health research money actually goes, saw the gap, and decided something as ordinary as a protein bar could start closing it.

There is a particular fear that arrives once you have watched it happen to someone. Not the fear of your body slowing down, that one you can almost make peace with. The fear of your mind going. Of forgetting a face you have loved your whole life. Of becoming a stranger in your own kitchen.

For a long time the culture treated that outcome as simple bad luck. A dice roll. Something that either finds you or does not, and nothing you do in the decades before makes much difference. That belief is comforting in one way and quietly devastating in another, because it tells you there is nothing to be done, so you may as well not think about it.

But sit with the research even briefly and a stranger fact jumps out, one most people have never been told. Alzheimer’s is not evenly distributed. Roughly two out of every three people living with it are women. It is not a rounding error. It is a landslide.

A disease that lands overwhelmingly on women, studied for decades mostly on everyone but them. Once you see that gap you cannot unsee it.

And here is the part that reframes the fear entirely. The brain is not a sealed box that fate either spares or does not. It is tissue. Hungry, demanding, high-maintenance tissue, and like the rest of you it is built and maintained from what you give it, over years, quietly, long before any symptom would ever show.

Which turns that helpless dice-roll feeling into something else: a set of levers. Not a cure, nobody honest is promising a cure. But the boring, powerful truth that the raw materials a brain runs on are things you can actually choose to supply, or not, every single day for decades.

That was the thought a mother and son could not put down. She had watched what memory loss does to a family up close. He was young enough that the decades of prevention were all still in front of him. And between them sat a simple, almost annoyingly humble question: if the brain is built from what you feed it, why is almost nothing you can actually eat built around feeding it?

So they built one. Not a supplement you forget in a drawer. Something you would reach for anyway, on a normal Tuesday, that happened to be engineered around the nutrients the research keeps pointing back to. What those nutrients are, and the reason a bar was the vehicle instead of a pill, is where the mission turns into a method.
