What Americans call ‘spicy’ is a single note. She grew up eating the whole song.

A cook from Chengdu spent years trying to explain the flavor she was homesick for. Then she stopped explaining it and started bottling it.
By Harel FilibaUpdated July 20266 min read
Image direction
Warm editorial food photo. Close, moody overhead of a spoon lifting deep-red chili oil off the rim of a plain ceramic bowl, flecks of aromatics suspended in it, steam. Appetite-first, not product-catalog. No branded jar in frame.

The first time Jing tried to explain it to an American friend, she reached for the word ‘spicy’ and watched it fail in real time. Because the friend nodded, and reached for hot sauce, and Jing realized they were not talking about the same thing at all. Not even close.

She grew up in Chengdu, in the west of China, where heat is not a dare and not a single sensation. It is a construction. There is the chili, yes, but under it a numbing, buzzing tingle from Sichuan peppercorn that makes your lips hum, and around that a low savory depth, a sweetness, a fermented funk, an aroma that arrives before the burn does. A whole architecture of flavor the locals have a single word for: mala.

What she kept meeting in America was one note played loudly. Heat as volume. Hotter, hotter, a competition to survive the plate. And she understood the appeal, but it made her homesick in a specific way, because it was like moving somewhere that had music but only knew how to turn it up.

Spice, where she came from, was never about how much you could take. It was about how much was happening at once.

So she did the thing homesick cooks do. She tried to make it herself. And she ran straight into the reason nobody had simply bottled this and sold it: the good version is genuinely hard. The flavor lives almost entirely in the raw materials and the process, and both had been quietly cheapened out of nearly everything on the shelf.

The peppercorn that creates the tingle loses its life fast and the potent stuff is expensive, so most jars use a tired, muted version. The aromatics that build the depth take time and get skipped. What is left is chili and oil and salt, heat with the architecture stripped out, and it is exactly what had made her wave off the whole aisle for years.

Which left her with a choice most homesick people never get to make. Keep trying to explain a flavor that does not translate. Or stop translating, source the real thing at the quality it actually requires, and let people taste the sentence she had been failing to say out loud for years.

She chose the second one. And the single decision she made about where the peppercorn comes from, the one that makes the first bite land in a place no hot sauce ever reaches, is where this stops being a memory and starts being the reason.
