Why a genuinely good chef’s knife costs $200, and why it does not have to

Most home cooks quietly blame their own skills for the nightly chore of dinner prep. The honest culprit is usually the cheap mystery-steel in their hand.
By Harel FilibaUpdated July 20265 min read
Image direction
Appetite-first editorial photo. Close overhead of a sharp knife cleanly cutting through an onion into paper-thin, even slices on a wooden board, the cut pieces fanned out. Crisp, satisfying, tactile. No branding.

There is a specific little defeat that happens at the cutting board most nights. The onion that tears instead of slices. The tomato that gets crushed into pulp instead of cut. The chicken you end up sawing at. And the quiet conclusion, drawn a thousand times by people who cook, that they are just not very good at this.

Almost none of them suspect the knife. It is the one tool in the whole process nobody examines, because a knife is a knife, and the one in the block came with the set, and it looks basically like the ones chefs use on television. So the blame flows to the cook, where it does not belong.

Because here is what a dull, cheap knife actually does to you. It makes you press harder, which makes it slip, which makes it dangerous. It bruises everything it touches. It turns a task that should take two minutes into a small, joyless battle, and it does this every single night, quietly convincing you that cooking is a chore you are bad at rather than one your equipment is sabotaging.

A sharp, well-balanced knife does not make you a better cook. It reveals that you were never the problem.

So the reasonable person goes looking for a good knife and immediately hits the second trap: the price. Genuinely good chef’s knives routinely run well past two hundred dollars, and the assumption is that the steel simply costs that much. It does not. A large part of that number is the journey it takes to reach you.

A traditional knife passes through a chain of hands before it reaches yours, and each one takes a cut. The distributor, the importer, the retailer, the showroom. By the time it lands in the store, a large share of what you pay is the trip, not the tool. Sell the same quality straight from the maker to the kitchen, and that entire markup simply disappears.

What that leaves is a strange and slightly heretical possibility: a knife built to the standard that usually costs two hundred, for a fraction of it. How that is actually done, and the one spec that separates a knife worth keeping for life from a pretty one that dulls in a month, is where dinner stops being a chore.
