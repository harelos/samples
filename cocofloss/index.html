Your gums bleed for a reason, and it is not that you ‘floss too hard’

Two dentists got tired of watching patients floss every day and still turn up with the same quiet inflammation. The problem, they decided, was never the person. It was the string.
By Harel FilibaUpdated July 20265 min read

Clean, clinical-but-warm editorial photo. A dentist in her own office, mid-thirties, looking directly and a little wryly at the camera, arms crossed. Trustworthy, not stocky. Soft daylight. No product in frame.

Dr. Cu has had the same small, frustrating conversation several thousand times. A patient sits down. She checks the gums. They are inflamed, tender, bleeding a little at the edges, the early quiet signature of a problem that ends, years later, somewhere expensive. And the patient, before she can even ask, says it: I floss every day.

And the thing is, they are not lying. That is what took her years to fully accept. Most of the people with bleeding gums are flossing. Diligently. Nightly. Doing the exact thing they have been told their whole lives will prevent precisely the problem they now have.

The usual explanation, the one most of us have heard from behind the mask, is that we are doing it wrong. Too hard. Too fast. Snapping it down. And there is some truth in that, but it always bothered her, because it puts the failure back on a person who is, by every measure, trying. You flossed every day and still lost. Clearly your fault.

It is not your technique. In most cases it is not even close. The tool you were handed to do the job was designed to do a different job, quietly, decades ago.

Consider what standard floss actually is. A thin, flat, waxed filament, engineered above all to be slippery. That was the whole design brief: slide it between two tight teeth without shredding or getting stuck. Which it does beautifully. And that is exactly the problem.

Because slippery and clean are, mechanically, almost opposites. A smooth waxed strand slides past the film of bacteria clinging to the side of a tooth without ever really grabbing it. You feel the floss go in, you feel it come out, you see nothing dramatic, and you reasonably conclude the job is done. It is not. You have run a squeegee that never touched the glass.

So the same scene repeats in dental chairs everywhere. Millions of conscientious people doing the ritual exactly as instructed, night after night, and getting the inflammation anyway, and being told, gently, that they must be doing it wrong. When the real answer is that the string was optimized to slide, not to clean, and no amount of correct technique can make a squeegee into a scrub.

Which is the thought that eventually pulled Dr. Cu and her sister out of clinical practice and into a genuinely strange project. If the failure is not the person and not the technique but the physical surface of the floss itself, then the question is almost embarrassingly simple. What would you have to change about the string to make it actually grab what it is supposed to remove?

The answer, it turned out, had nothing to do with wax and everything to do with texture, with the number of fibers and the surface area they present. And the first time a patient came back after switching, the gums told the story before the patient said a word.
